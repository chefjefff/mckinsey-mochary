# Case Study: Event Plan Before & After

*A real before/after from a B2B sales team. Same event. Same budget. Same two hours of planning. Different operating system. Names and specifics generalized for portability.*

---

## The setup

A two-person B2B enterprise sales team had two events on the calendar in a single month:

1. A sponsored industry conference ($8K sponsorship, own booth, one team member speaking)
2. A co-hosted intimate dinner with a partner company (~30 CFOs/VP Finance leaders invited)

Both events had detailed, well-formatted plans already written — each about 140 lines, covering logistics, outreach timelines, talking points, budget, and ROI targets.

The original plans looked professional. They passed the "does this look like a plan?" eye test.

Then we ran the 3-question test from the triad.

---

## The "before" diagnostic

### Thinking layer — `/problem-solving` gaps

- **No clear problem statement.** The plans answered "how do we execute this event?" but not "what pipeline outcome does this event solve for, at what audience, at what cost, against what knock-out check?"
- **No cleaving frame applied.** Events could have been cleaved on audience quality (buyers vs vendors), warm/cold mix, pre-event/at-event/post-event conversion, strategic-relationship vs pipeline-dollar. Neither plan chose a frame.
- **No synthesis.** Both plans were lists of activities, not pyramid-structured arguments with a governing thought.

### Planning layer — `/work-plan` gaps

- **No top-level pipeline hypothesis.** Just an ROI target at the bottom of the doc ("4-6 SQLs, 2-3 SQOs in 60 days"). No "because..." tying the activities to the outcome.
- **No knock-out analysis.** For the conference, the highest-leverage early check was "is this audience actually populated with buyers, or mostly vendors and consultants?" — not in the plan. For the dinner, the knock-out was "can we realistically fill 15 seats with senior finance leaders, given that typical CFO cold-outreach reply rates are under 10%?" — also not in the plan.
- **Tasks were logistics, not analyses.** "Update QR code", "print 1-pagers", "confirm session time" — activities, not analyses with hypothesis + owner + output.
- **Post-event workplan was 7 bullets.** This is the most pipeline-critical phase of the entire event. It was the thinnest section of both plans.
- **Due dates had no times.** "This week", "day of", "after the event" — all vague.
- **No funnel projection.** No math from invitations → RSVPs → attendance → quality conversations → **meetings booked AT the event** → SQLs → SQOs → closed.
- **No kill list.** What are we explicitly NOT doing, and why? Silent.
- **No risk register.** What if the audience is mostly vendors? What if the co-host delivers fewer attendees than expected? What if the RSVPs skew junior? Silent.

### Operating layer — `/mochary-method` gaps

- **No single DRI per line.** "Jeffrey/Prab rotate 45-min shifts" — who owns the booth conversion quality check? Both? Neither? Unclear.
- **No Issue/Proposed Solution framing.** Risks were implied but not paired with proposed actions + owners + dates.
- **No Fear/Anger check on the sunk cost.** $8K sponsorship paid — if the audience turned out to be mostly vendors, would the team downgrade effort or push through?
- **No Top Goal protection for post-event follow-up.** The 8am-12pm window the week after the event — the highest-leverage pipeline-converting block — wasn't calendar-blocked.

**Diagnosis:** both plans would have generated *some* pipeline, but a large fraction of the investment would have evaporated silently through missed knock-outs, weak at-event conversion mechanics, and under-prioritized follow-up.

---

## The "after" — same plans, rebuilt through the triad

### What changed for the conference plan

1. **Pipeline hypothesis stated explicitly:**
   > *"If we sponsor + speak at this conference with a conversion-oriented booth and disciplined post-event follow-up, we will generate 4-6 SQLs and 2-3 SQOs within 60 days, producing $150-300K in qualified pipeline — because Max's session positions us as a complement (not a competitor) to existing PCPs, the audience is actively shopping for prevention solutions, and our own booth + speaker slot gives us 3+ touch points per qualified attendee vs 1 for typical sponsors."*

2. **Knock-out analysis sequenced FIRST** (before any other prep):
   > *"Is this audience actually populated with self-insured benefits decision-makers, or is it heavy on vendors/consultants? If <30% are buyers, no booth strategy or follow-up saves the math. Better to know now and re-allocate the team's time than discover it on event day. Owner: [name]. Due: [date + 5pm]. Output: one-line read-back with % buyer/vendor/consultant split + go/no-go decision."*

3. **SCR one-day answer** capturing where the event sits in the broader pipeline picture.

4. **Explicit funnel with conversion rates** at every stage:
   - 100-150 total attendees → 30-60 qualified buyers (pending knock-out) → 25-40 booth conversations → 12-20 quality conversations → **5-8 meetings booked AT the event** → 4-6 held (SQLs) → 2-3 SQOs → 0-1 closed

5. **Three 6-column workplans** — pre-event, at-event, AND post-event — with the post-event workplan being the longest section. Post-event is where pipeline is made; it got the most ink.

6. **At-event conversion mechanic codified** (qualify in 60 sec → demo 90 sec → **make the ask and send the calendar invite IN THE MOMENT** → voice memo after).

7. **Named DRI + date + time** on every line. "Prep at 9am" became "booth setup begins 9:00 AM, Jeffrey + Prab, complete by 10:30 AM, banners + demo + materials live."

8. **Kill list** — no pitch decks at booth, no follow-up to non-buyers, no general "stop by" outreach, no competing for host's attention during their event.

9. **Risk register** with mitigations — audience quality risk (mitigated by knock-out analysis), booked meetings no-show risk (mitigated by sending calendar invite in the moment), follow-up deprioritization risk (mitigated by calendar-blocking 8am-12pm for 3 days post-event, treated as inviolable).

### What changed for the dinner plan

1. **Pipeline hypothesis stated explicitly:**
   > *"If we co-host an intimate wellness experience with [partner] for 30 senior finance + benefits decision-makers and execute disciplined at-event + post-event mechanics, we will book 5-7 follow-up meetings and create 2-3 SQOs within 30 days, producing $200-400K in qualified pipeline — because the casual format enables peer-level conversations that don't trigger sales-defensive posture, complimentary membership creates immediate product experience, and the co-host's brand halo gives us instant credibility with CFOs."*

2. **Knock-out analysis:**
   > *"Can we realistically fill 15 seats with senior decision-makers (CFO, VP Finance, Sr Director Benefits or higher), or will we compromise on seniority to hit headcount? Filling with 15 junior people destroys the pipeline math. First read by [date +8] evening, final go/no-go by [date +11] 5pm."*

3. **Conversion mechanic for the event night:**
   - Open with curiosity, not company ("How do you take care of yourself?")
   - Find the pain in their org ("What are you actually doing for your team's health benefits right now?")
   - Use the membership as bridge ("You already have membership tonight — use it. If it lands, I'd love to walk your team through what we're doing for [X].")
   - **Make the ask before they walk away** — "Are you free Wed or Thu next week for 30 min? Let me send a calendar hold right now."
   - Voice memo within 5 min of the conversation

4. **Day-of workplan** for 4 SP staff with specific role assignments (greeter, welcome remarks, room-workers) and real-time coordination protocols.

5. **Post-event workplan with 11 specific tasks** including a "did you try your membership?" check-in 5-7 days post-event — previously missing entirely.

6. **Risk register** including: Tier 1 warm RSVP rate <50% (mitigated by Apr 22 knock-out), co-host delivers <10 attendees (mitigated by Apr 16 confirmation ask), senior CFO cancels day-of (mitigated by 5-person waitlist activated within 12h), conversion mechanic feels too salesy (mitigated by practicing Apr 30 and calibrating language).

## What the triad actually changed

| Dimension | Before | After |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Top-level output | Logistics checklist | Hypothesis-driven bet |
| Sequencing | Tasks in time order | Tasks in **knock-out** order |
| Due dates | "This week" | "Wed Apr 16, 5pm PT" |
| Owners | "Jeffrey/Prab" | Single named DRI per line |
| Post-event section | 7 bullets | 10-11 structured tasks with DRIs + dates |
| At-event goal | "Staff the booth" / "Run the dinner" | "5-8 meetings booked DURING the event" |
| Risk handling | Implicit | Explicit register with mitigations |
| What we're NOT doing | Unstated | Kill list |
| Synthesis | Narrative | Pyramid with governing thought |

The inputs were the same. The team was the same. The two hours of planning time were the same. But the *operating system* behind the plan was different, and that turned logistics into a bet with named owners, conversion rates, and a protected follow-up window.

---

## Takeaways for your own use

1. **Your existing plans are probably logistics checklists in disguise.** Run the 3-question test on the last plan you wrote. Most fail at least one question. That's the starting point.

2. **The knock-out check is the highest-leverage addition.** If you do nothing else, identify the one thing that — if wrong — kills the project. Sequence it first. Give it a named owner and a deadline.

3. **Post-execution workplans are where pipeline is made or lost.** Your event/campaign/launch plan should have its longest section AFTER the event, not before. If the pre-event section is the thickest, you're planning logistics, not outcomes.

4. **Dates without times are fictions.** "By Friday" loses you a day. "Fri Apr 25, 5pm PT" is a commitment.

5. **One DRI per line. No exceptions.** "The team will handle it" means nobody will.

6. **The 3-question test is the gate.** Before handing over any plan: does it pass Thinking, Planning, and Operating? If any answer is no, rewrite before shipping.
